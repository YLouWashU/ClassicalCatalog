Editor's Choice
Florence Price

Symphonies Nos. 1 & 3

Philadelphia Orchestra / Yannick Nézet-Séguin · DG · 486 1900
Yannick Nézet-Séguin delivers a revelatory interpretation of Florence Price's two symphonies, bringing 'body and soul' to music that has languished in neglect for nearly a century. With the Philadelphia Orchestra at peak form, the performance captures both the deep sorrow and defiant joy of Black experience in early 20th-century America. The Second Symphony's second subject possesses an authentic 'homespun quality' that renders even Dvořák a mere tourist in comparison; the Largo movement stands as a timeless funeral procession 'not out of place on the streets of New Orleans today.' Nézet-Séguin's mission is clear: not to treat Price as a footnote but to reveal her as a composer of 'great honesty and truth,' full of 'infectious joy in the unquenchable spirit of her brothers and sisters.' Wind solos are poetic, brass chorales are majestic, and the entire recording radiates with discovery rather than mere advocacy. An essential document that poses the haunting question: had the world been different, what might Florence Price have become?

Bach

Six Brandenburg Concertos, BWV1046-1051

Isabelle Faust vn Antoine Tamestit va / Akademie für Alte Musik Berlin · Harmonia Mundi · HMM90 2686/7
Isabelle Faust and Antoine Tamestit join the Akademie für Alte Musik Berlin in a celebration of communal music-making, this recording marking the ensemble's 30th anniversary with 10 members from their 1997 version. The performances radiate genuine joy, with Faust's introspective retreats and surging returns in No 4 showcasing her distinctive musical voice, while Forck and Kallweit handle the remaining violin roles admirably. The period instrument standards have advanced considerably since 1997, evident in No 2's subtler shaping and natural balance that brings swirling energy. No 6 features larger-than-life violas enabling warmly expressive duetting, while No 3 flies swiftly with deft articulation allowing every contrapuntal line to live and shine. Huntgeburth's gorgeous flute in No 5 and the effective harpsichord cadenza pause add further distinction. Lindsay Kemp praises this as a real winner for its sense of shared enjoyment.

Barber, Rachmaninov

Cello Sonatas Op. 6 & Op. 19, songs

Sheku Kanneh‑Mason (cello), Isata Kanneh‑Mason (piano) · Decca · F 485 1630
Both Kanneh‑Mason siblings approach this programme with the intimacy of a private conversation and the virtuosity of seasoned soloists. Sheku’s singing tone on the Barber Cello Sonata blends warmth and drama, while Isata’s piano provides lithe, colour‑rich accompaniment. The Rachmaninov Sonata unfolds as an inexhaustible cascade of melody, the duo capturing the work’s Romantic grandeur yet never losing sight of its lyrical core. Interspersed song transcriptions – Barber’s lullaby‑like ‘A Slumber Song of the Madonna’ and Rachmaninov’s haunting ‘The Muse’ – add varied textures and showcase the siblings’ ability to make even the smallest pieces breathe. The recording’s immediacy and natural balance give the music a live‑recital presence, making this disc the most enterprising and emotionally compelling among recent releases.

Ran Dank’s disc interleaves William Bolcom’s twelve newly composed Études with Chopin’s Mazurkas, Polonaises and Waltz, creating a provocative dialogue between contemporary American voice and Romantic piano literature. Throughout, Dank’s dynamic palette ranges from delicate PPP to powerful FF, and his rubato is liberal yet controlled. The result is a distinctive, thought‑provoking recital that rewards repeated listening.

Nostalgia

Magdalena Kožená, Yefim Bronfman · Pentatone · PTC5186
Magdalena Kožená and Yefim Bronfman deliver a captivating programme titled Nostalgia that spans Bartók’s Village Scenes, a selection of Brahms songs and Mussorgsky’s The Nursery. Kožená’s voice, praised for its radiant tone and spontaneous immediacy, captures the folk‑inflected drama of Bartók’s miniature rustic scenes, the tender longing of Brahms’s lieder, and the unsentimental wit of Mussorgsky’s childhood vignettes. Bronfman’s piano playing is bold and colourfully textured, providing the perfect foil for Kožená’s nuanced phrasing. Together they illuminate how each composer transforms folk material into deeply personal expression. The mezzo’s comic flair shines in the Mussorgsky, while her pure, soprano‑like lightness in the high register brings an endearing innocence to the young Misha. The recording balances truth and beauty, offering an emotionally rich journey that reveals new facets of familiar repertoire. The partnership between Kožená and Bronfman is a model of collaborative artistry, making this disc essential for lovers of art song.

Josquin S Missa Hercules Dux Ferrarie 1503 4

This feature article by Fabrice Fitch, published in Gramophone's November 2021 issue, explores Josquin's Missa Hercules dux Ferrarie (1503/4), a Renaissance mass written for Duke Ercole I d'Este of Ferrara. The mass derives its title from an eight-note ostinato based on the syllables of the Duke's name and rank (Hercules dux Ferrarie = re ut re ut re fa mi re), creating a severe yet inventive geometric structure. The article serves as a guided musical journey through the work and its influence, organized into five thematic sections: the mass itself alongside Josquin's other ostinato masses (La sol fa re mi, Ut Phebi radiis, Faisant regretz); Renaissance homages such as Cipriano de Rore's and Escobedo's masses; Baroque references including Frescobaldi's capriccio and Bach's Musikalisches Opfer; modern examples like Berg's Kammerkonzert and Boulez's Messagesquisse; and a wild card pairing the Brahms/Schumann/Dietrich 'FAE' Sonata. The Hilliard Ensemble's recording is singled out as the pick of countertenor-led versions.

Out Of The Shadows

This Gramophone feature article examines the resurgence of interest in Florence Price (1887-1973), the pioneering African American composer who was the first woman of African descent to have her symphony performed by a major American orchestra (the Chicago Symphony in 1933). Author Andrew Farach-Colton explores new research by scholars Dr. Samantha Ege and Professor Douglas Shadle that reveals how Price's achievement was not a solitary breakthrough but part of a robust network of educated Black women, notably Maude Roberts George, who worked behind the scenes to support and promote Price's music. The article discusses Price's compositional style, arguing that while her symphonies superficially appear conservative, they actually incorporate distinctly African American musical elements including call-and-response phrases, African drums, and spirituals—a transformative moment when plantation music asserted itself in the concert hall. Scholars highlight her influence from Samuel Coleridge-Taylor rather than simply Dvořák, and her willingness to draw from musical soundscapes outside the orchestral realm. Conductor Yannick Nézet-Séguin praises her personal voice and confidence in manipulating symphonic form, noting the unusually long first two movements across her three surviving symphonies.

Sibling Musings

This Gramophone feature from November 2021 profiles cellist Sheku Kanneh-Mason and pianist-sister Isata Kanneh-Mason discussing their debut duo album 'Muse' for Decca. The recording features two substantial cello sonatas: Rachmaninoff's beloved Sonata in G minor (composed 1901, celebrating its 120th anniversary in 2021) and Samuel Barber's comparatively rarer Sonata from 1932. Beyond the sonatas, the album includes arrangements of songs by both composers—Barber's songs arranged by Simon Parkin, and Rachmaninoff's songs adapted by simply playing the vocal top line an octave lower on cello. The siblings discuss their approach to capturing a live performance energy in the studio, praising producer Jonathan Allen's method of using large sections rather than meticulous patching. Sheku shares his admiration for historical artists like Piatigorsky, Casals, and pianist Frederic Lamond, while Isata mentions her influences including Artur Schnabel, Horowitz, and Martha Argerich. The interview took place the day after their grandfather's passing, highlighting their remarkable professionalism. The article also provides background on the remarkable Kanneh-Mason family—seven siblings all pursuing music—with details about their childhood home featuring four pianos where siblings practiced simultaneously.

Soprano On The Edge

This Gramophone feature from November 2021 profiles Russian soprano Anna Netrebko, exploring her artistic evolution from light soubrette roles to dramatic Wagnerian and Verdian parts. The article examines her new album "Amata dalle tenebre" (Beloved by the Shadows), recorded live at La Scala under Riccardo Chailly. Netrebko discusses how her voice naturally developed after the birth of her son in 2008, necessitating a move to heavier repertoire that expanded her breath control and technical capabilities. The album includes music by Wagner (Elisabeth, Elsa, Isolde), Purcell (Dido's Lament), Strauss, and other composers—all pieces united by their confrontation with mortality and darkness. Despite initial skepticism about ever singing Wagner, Netrebko embraced the challenge, studying recordings of legendary sopranos like Birgit Nilsson and Nina Stemme. The feature also touches on the pandemic's impact on her career and her recovery from Covid-19, while tracing her remarkable journey from the Kirov Opera's 2000 War and Peace production through her signature roles as Violetta, Lady Macbeth, and Turandot. The article provides insight into an artist unafraid to push beyond her comfort zone as she enters a new creative phase.

Willem Mengelberg

This Gramophone feature commemorates the 150th anniversary of Dutch conductor Willem Mengelberg's birth, portraying him as a legendary 'icon of icons' whose recorded legacy remains deeply influential. Appointed Principal Conductor of the Concertgebouw Orchestra in 1895 at just 24, Mengelberg held the position until 1945, collaborating closely with Mahler and premiering works by Bartók and Kodály. Described as 'a Napoleon of the orchestra' and 'a sort of Horowitz of the orchestra,' he was known as an autocrat and perfectionist whose rehearsal style was obsessively verbose yet whose performances were magnetic to the point of hypnosis. The article examines his distinctive Mahler interpretations, noting his 1939 Concertgebouw broadcast of the Fourth Symphony sounds almost identical to Mahler's own gestures, and his dramatic 1939 St Matthew Passion, which he transformed into a 'full-blown opera.' Beyond Mahler, Cowan highlights exceptional recordings of Tchaikovsky (the Pathétique with progressively slower finales), Brahms's Fourth Symphony (1938) with uniquely drawn-out inner string lines, and praises Mengelberg's 1928 Ein Heldenleben as remaining 'unchallenged.' The article also addresses controversy surrounding his conducting during Nazi occupation, for which he was banned from the Netherlands. Recommended recordings are available through Pristine Audio, Naxos, and online at willemmengelberg.nl.

Ysa E S Violin Sonata No 2

This Gramophone article features violinist Jack Liebeck discussing Eugene Ysaÿe's Violin Sonata No. 2 in A minor from 1923. Liebeck, a busy concerto soloist and chamber musician, explains his long-standing obsession with Ysaÿe’s six solo violin sonatas, contrasting them favorably with solo Bach, which he finds "horribly written for the violin." He describes Ysaÿe’s sonatas as fitting "in the hand" for violinists with sufficient technique. The article explores the drama of each movement: the first "Obsession" begins with a Bach quotation before disintegrating into anger; the second "Malinconia" requires a mute to color the sound; the third "Danse des ombres" features pizzicato playing like piano technique; and the finale "Les furies" contains dazzling outbursts and sul ponticello passages. Liebeck describes Ysaÿe’s music as the "violin equivalent of Jimi Hendrix"—raw, cutting-edge, unashamed, slightly kitsch, but wonderful. He performs on a 1785 'Wilhelmj' Guadagnini violin that "needs a player to ignite it." The article references his Ysaÿe recording reviewed on page 62 of the same issue.
