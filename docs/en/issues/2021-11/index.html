Recording of the Month
Florence Price

Symphonies No. 1 & No. 3

Philadelphia Orchestra / Yannick Nézet‑Séguin · Deutsche Grammophon · DG F D 486 1900
Recording of the Month: Florence Price’s First and Third Symphonies receive revelatory performances under Yannick Nézet‑Séguin and the Philadelphia Orchestra. The conductor treats the scores as new discoveries, revealing Price’s distinctive voice—homespun melodies fused with African‑American spirituals, powerful brass chorales and a joy that refuses to be overshadowed by historical neglect. Nézet‑Séguin’s direction is urgent yet tender, letting the orchestra’s wind solos glow and the brass erupt with dignity, especially in the Mahlerian funeral march of the Second Symphony’s Largo and the exuberant Juba dances that celebrate improvised percussion. The Philadelphia strings produce a warm, full‑bodied sound that captures both sorrow and defiant optimism. Nézet‑Séguin’s approach balances the music’s sorrow with an unapologetic joy, allowing the listener to hear the depth of Price’s emotional truth. The recording’s engineering highlights the orchestra’s dynamic range, from delicate woodwind whispers to thunderous brass climaxes. The result is a compelling case for Price’s symphonies as essential repertoire, not a footnote in music history.

Editor's Choice
Florence Price

Symphonies Nos. 1 & 3

Philadelphia Orchestra / Yannick Nézet‑Séguin · DG
Martin Cullingford’s pick celebrates a long‑overdue revival, and the Philadelphia Orchestra under Yannick Nézet‑Séguin delivers a performance that does full justice to Florence Price’s inventive voice. From the opening bars the orchestra’s refined woodwinds and warm brass delineate Price’s thematic ideas with remarkable clarity, while the strings produce a luminous tone that underlines the music’s lyrical charm. Nézet‑Séguin’s reading balances gravitas and optimism, shaping the first symphony’s developmental drama with a sure sense of pacing and the third’s suite‑like contrast with an infectious lightness. The orchestra’s dynamic range is exploited fully, from whispered pianissimos to thunderous climaxes, while Nézet‑Séguin’s tempi give each movement a natural breathing space that lets Price’s melodic invention shine. The recording’s engineering captures the hall’s natural reverb, giving weight to the lower registers and sparkle to the upper. The result is a triumphant document that proves Price’s symphonies are not merely historic curiosities but vital, expressive works worthy of the repertoire.

Editor's Choice
Bartók, Beethoven, Berg

Violin Concertos

Frank Peter Zimmermann, violin; Berlin Philharmonic Orchestra
Frank Peter Zimmermann brings his celebrated combination of technical brilliance and interpretive depth to a programme that unites three divergent violin concertos. In Bartók’s single‑movement work his phrasing is razor‑sharp, the instrument’s edge tempered by a warm, honeyed tone that captures the folk‑like urgency without aggression. The Beethoven concerto receives a reading of classical poise and Romantic warmth, the orchestra’s refined accompaniment providing a transparent backdrop that lets Zimmermann’s clean articulation shine. Berg’s lyrical effusion is rendered with hushed intensity, the violinist’s sustain and subtle vibrato drawing out the music’s inner grief. Throughout, the Berlin Philharmonic’s precision and luminous texture guarantee a support that is both robust and responsive, while the recording balances soloist and ensemble with natural depth. The result is a cohesive traversal that highlights Zimmermann’s ability to make each composer’s voice distinct yet organically related. Zimmermann’s musical intelligence and the orchestra’s polished execution make this a benchmark recording for the genre.

Beethoven

Symphony No. 5, Op. 67

Philharmonia Zurich / Nikolaus Harnoncourt · Prospero · PROSP0020
Harnoncourt's third recording of Beethoven's Fifth is his most refined, dispensing with the fragmentary quirks of his Concentus Musicus version to deliver a reading that is faster, lighter, and more heroic than ever before. What distinguishes this Zurich live performance is its avoidance of the valedictory gloom that often haunts late-career interpretations—instead, the symphony unfolds with blazing life-affirmation. The Scherzo's transformation into its fugal Trio, with those characteristic Luftpausen, now feels integral rather than mannered. The slow movement receives particular attention, its long lines shaped with consummate skill. Harnoncourt's Mozart Gran Partita is equally distinguished: the Molto allegro opening balances meaningful pauses with propulsive energy, the slow movement flows with warm orchestral blend, and the finale rushes attacca into the work's extrovert conclusion. This double bill serves as testament to a conductor who redefined orchestral music's relationship with historically informed performance.

Berg

Violin Concerto

Frank Peter Zimmermann; Berlin Philharmonic / Kirill Petrenko · Berliner Philharmoniker · BPHR210151
This live Berlin recording transforms Berg's 'tribute to the memory of an angel' into something truly exceptional. Zimmermann and Petrenko uncover a 'dancing angel' quality—where earlier readings under Gelmetti present the work's two emotional halves (fantastical recollection followed by heartbreaking reflection) more conventionally, here the Viennese aspect emerges with unprecedented clarity, the music's variegated tonal canvas ranging from threatening tower blocks to kneeling supplication. The second movement's journey to the Bach reference and world of prayer is navigated with aching tenderness. What makes this version indispensable is its rare combination of musical refinement and tragedian depth—Zimmermann's tonal range and Petrenko's attentiveness to every phrase elevate this above competition from Tetzlaff, Zehetmair, and Menuhin. The surrounding Bartók concertos are equally compelling: the First's first movement is 'tonally silken and shaped with the hypnotic skill of a Celibidache' (better than Stern/Bernstein), while the Second 'darts and dances on the wings of youthful love.' This box set, with its handsome presentation including Blu-ray, is the Berg you go to first.

Barber / Rachmaninov

Cello Sonata Op 6; Cello Sonata Op 19 (with song transcriptions)

Sheku Kanneh-Mason (cello), Isata Kanneh-Mason (piano) · Decca · F 485 1630
Barber and Rachmaninov may seem an unlikely pairing, but siblings Sheku and Isata Kanneh‑Mason make a compelling case for their program of two cello sonatas flanked by song transcriptions. Sheku’s playing in the Barber Sonata combines youthful vigor with an innate sense of drama, deploying a warm, singing tone and a commanding lower register that brings the work’s finale to life. In the Rachmaninov, his tenor‑range excursions and hypnotic pianissimo reveal a poetic depth that rivals the legendary Jacqueline du Pré, while Isata’s piano provides a luminous, supportive backdrop. The Decca recording captures the duo’s intimate synergy with immediacy, preserving fine dynamic nuance and a natural balance between instruments. The song transcriptions, arranged by Simon Parkin and Sheku himself, are gems of brevity and expressiveness, especially “There’s nae lark” and “How fair this spot”. This disc stands out for its enterprising repertoire and the siblings’ consummate musicianship, truly remarkable and unforgettable.

William Bolcom, Frédéric Chopin

Bolcom Twelve New Études, Chopin Mazurkas, Polonaises, Waltzes

Ran Dank (piano) · Avie · AV2475
Israeli-born pianist Ran Dank's debut album offers a provocative programming concept: interspersing William Bolcom's Twelve New Études with works of Chopin, creating intriguing musical dialogues between the two composer-pianists. Dank proves equally stimulating as pianist and programme-builder, with a brisk, hair-trigger approach to Bolcom's Études that dovetails naturally into Chopin pieces like the Op 42 Waltz and A minor Mazurka. His treatment of Chopin's C minor Polonaise as a brooding operatic prologue rather than a dance sets up Bolcom's first Étude as shockingly effective contrast. The recording captures a distinctive and thoughtful artistic voice—one who notices the remnants of Chopin's A flat major key in Bolcom's seventh Étude, and brings personal poetry to the A flat Polonaise without resorting to Horowitz-style swagger. Excellent engineering and highly recommended debut.

Frédéric Chopin

Études, Op 25. Four Scherzos

Beatrice Rana (piano) · Warner Classics · 9029 67642-4
Rising star Beatrice Rana delivers a technically fearless Chopin performance marked by fire, poetry, imagination, and temperament. Her 2019 Carnegie Hall appearance earned rapturous New York press coverage, and this studio debut transfers much of that live, big-hall presence to the recording. While some Op 25 Études sound over-pedalled—perhaps suitable for a concert hall but less ideal for home listening—and the Scherzos swing between epic power and intimate confiding that nearly threatens coherence, the large-scale repeats showcase distinguished subtlety of voicing and timing. Rana's booklet note reveals her interpretive approach, and what some might perceive as overblown grandeur in the Études reflects her deliberate architecture, directing the twelve studies toward climactic mid-point and end-point goals. Fine recording quality and a pianist who will repay repeated listening.

Nico Muhly

Nico Muhly – Rapturous Music (featuring Renée Fleming)

Renée Fleming, Nico Muhly
Renée Fleming’s collaboration with Nico Muhly on this album reveals her legendary luminous soprano to Muhly’s contemporary song cycle, delivering a performance of rare poise and emotional immediacy. Muhly’s ravishing, often multilayered textures provide Fleming with ample room to showcase her radiant tone, which effortlessly fills every phrase with longing, wit and introspection. Her diction is crystal clear, allowing Muhly’s clever word-settings to shine, while her interpretive instincts turn each movement into a vivid narrative. Particularly impressive is her ability to balance the cerebral themes of the music with genuine warmth, never letting the intellectual surface overshadow the heart. The partnership between singer and composer feels mutually inspired, resulting in an account that feels both intimate and expansive. For listeners seeking a modern vocal disc that honours tradition while venturing into uncharted territory, Fleming’s latest is an essential addition.

Bellini

I Puritani

Lawrence Brownlee (tenor), Sarah Coburn (soprano), Azamat Zheltyrguzov (baritone), Kaunas State Choir, Kaunas City Symphony Orchestra / Constantine Orbelian · Delos · DE3537
Tenor Lawrence Brownlee delivers what the reviewer calls 'probably the most effortless Arturo on disc since Pavarotti' on this 2017 Lithuanian recording. His technically flawless performance showcases impeccable intonation, sweet bright timbre, and a thrilling approach to the infamous high F at Act 3's end—blending chest voice rather than resorting to falsetto like Pavarotti. Soprano Sarah Coburn matches him with clean coloratura technique and affecting moments, particularly in the Act 2 mad scene. While conductor Constantine Orbelian's stolid support and the dull engineering disappoint, Brownlee and Coburn's elegant phrasing and vocal control make this a worthwhile showcase for these two American bel canto specialists, even if it doesn't dislodge classic recommendations.

Icons Willem Mengelberg

This article celebrates the 150th anniversary of Dutch conductor Willem Mengelberg's birth, profiling him as one of the greatest conducting 'icons of icons.' Author Rob Cowan examines Mengelberg's remarkable ability to channel the spirit of composers like Mahler, noting how his 1939 Concertgebouw broadcast of Mahler's Fourth Symphony closely mirrors the composer's own conducting gestures. The article explores Mengelberg's extensive recorded legacy spanning Mahler, Bach (including a remarkable 1939 St Matthew Passion), Tchaikovsky (with his progressively slowing Pathétique finale across three recordings), Brahms, Beethoven, Wagner, Liszt, and Richard Strauss. Mengelberg's conducting style is described as autocratic and perfectionist, yet hypnotic and emotionally immediate—qualities that made his performances instantly appealing to audiences. Despite controversy surrounding his wartime conduct and subsequent exile from the Netherlands (1945-1951), Mengelberg's recordings continue to captivate listeners with their dramatic flair, emotional depth, and technical precision. The article particularly recommends his 1928 recording of Strauss's Ein Heldenleben as a defining example of his artistry.

The Musician And The Score

This feature article centers on violinist Jack Liebeck's interpretation of Ysaÿe's six unaccompanied violin sonatas, with particular focus on the Second Sonata in A minor. Liebeck discusses his longstanding obsession with these late-Romantic works, contrasting them with his admitted discomfort with solo Bach. He explores the first movement 'Obsession,' which opens with a Bach quotation and contains a possibly mocking reference to violinist Jacques Thibaud. The article delves into Liebeck's philosophy of personal expression over imitation, examining the Dies irae plainchant motif, the muted 'Malinconia' movement, the pizzicato sarabande 'Danse des ombres,' and the fiery finale 'Les furies.' Liebeck characterises Ysaÿe's music as the violin equivalent of Jimi Hendrix—raw, cutting-edge, unashamed, yet wonderful, describing fireworks, mystery and pure drama.
