Features

Icons Willem Mengelberg

This article celebrates the 150th anniversary of Dutch conductor Willem Mengelberg's birth, presenting him as a legendary 'icon of icons' in the conducting world. Mengelberg (1871-1951) is described as physically diminutive but magnetically commanding, known for his verbose rehearsals and Romantic musical approach. His extensive recorded legacy is praised for its emotional intensity and idiosyncratic phrasing. The article highlights his close relationship with Mahler, noting how his 1939 Concertgebouw broadcast of the Fourth Symphony closely echoes Mahler's own conducting style. Mengelberg's dramatic interpretations extended to Bach's St Matthew Passion, which he transformed into what the author describes as a 'full-blown opera,' and his work with the New York Philharmonic, where he shared the podium with his rival Arturo Toscanini. His career ended controversially after World War II due to his conduct during the Nazi occupation of the Netherlands, though the article focuses primarily on his musical achievements and recordings. The author recommends several recordings including Tchaikovsky's Romeo and Juliet overture (1930), various Mahler works, and notably Richard Strauss's Ein Heldenleben as his essential recording.

The Musician And The Score

This Gramophone feature profiles violinist Jack Liebeck discussing Eugène Ysaÿe's Violin Sonata No. 2 in A minor, one of six unaccompanied violin sonatas composed in July 1923. Liebeck, known as a collaborative musician and concerto soloist, explains his lifelong obsession with these works, which he finds more comfortable to play than solo Bach—admitting controversially that he finds Bach "horribly written for the violin." The sonatas were written by a violinist for violinists, offering lyrical freedom and technical grace that fits the instrument naturally. The interview explores the dramatic structure of the Second Sonata, particularly the first movement "Obsession," which begins with a Bach quotation and includes the Dies irae plainchant emerging from Bach-like passagework. Liebeck discusses his interpretive approach to the mournful second movement "Malinconia," requiring a mute, describing how he seeks "non-violin sounds" like distant graveyards or breezes. The third movement "Danse des ombres" features pizzicato playing likened to piano technique, while the finale "Les furies" unleashes jagged double-stops and whirling string-crossing passages. Liebeck celebrates Ysaÿe as a "mountainous, godlike figure" whose music conjures fireworks, mystery, and magic—describing it as the violin equivalent of Jimi Hendrix: raw, cutting-edge, unashamed, and dramatic.
