Recording of the Month
Florence Price

Symphonies Nos. 1 & 3

Philadelphia Orchestra / Yannick Nézet-Séguin · DG · F D 486 1900
Yannick Nézet-Séguin and the Philadelphia Orchestra deliver a revelatory account of Florence Price's First and Third Symphonies, music rescued from nearly a century of neglect. The conductor approaches these works with absolute commitment, treating them as new discoveries rather than historical curiosities. The orchestra brings 'fire, spontaneity and poetry' to passages that balance profound sorrow with defiant joy—particularly in the second movement of the First Symphony's post-Mahlerian funeral procession and the jubilant Juba dance rhythms. Nézet-Séguin emphasizes the composer's unique harmonic language and melodic development, allowing the violas their moments of quiet revelation while navigating unexpected twists like the trumpet-led coda of the First Symphony's finale. The brass chorales that recur throughout both works receive especially powerful treatment, their 'signature' sound conveying dignity and resolve. This recording—initially download-only, with CD release in January—proves Price's music is far more than a footnote; it demands attention as essential repertoire, performed with the conviction this music has always deserved.

Editor's Choice
Florence Price

Symphonies Nos. 1 & 3

Philadelphia Orchestra / Yannick Nézet‑Séguin · DG
For too long the symphonies of Florence Price languished in obscurity, but this DG recording restores them with a vengeance. Yannick Nézet‑Séguin draws the Philadelphia Orchestra into a reading that is both meticulously detailed and emotionally sweeping, revealing the inventive thematic architecture Price embedded in her First and Third Symphonies. The orchestral blend is luminous, the brass punch with authority, and the strings sing with a warmth that lets Price’s lyrical voice shine. Nézet‑Séguin’s tempo choices strike a perfect balance between urgency and reflection, allowing the music’s drama to unfold naturally while never overwhelming the delicate passages. The recording captures the hall’s natural reverb, giving the woodwinds and percussion a presence that feels intimate yet grand. The engineers have balanced the solo sections with the tutti so that each voice is distinct without becoming brittle. Moreover, Nézet‑Séguin’s commitment to Price’s cultural heritage brings a sensitivity that honors the composer’s original intent while making the music accessible to modern ears. This is not merely an act of rescue; it is a triumphant vindication of Price’s place in the canon, the kind of disc that makes you wonder why she was ever neglected.

Bartók, Beethoven, Berg

Violin Concertos

Frank Peter Zimmermann, Berlin Philharmonic Orchestra / Berliner Philharmoniker
Frank Peter Zimmermann’s traversal of the Bartók, Beethoven and Berg violin concertos is nothing short of riveting. In the Bartók Second Concerto he brings a bone‑dry, rhythmically razor‑sharp sound that matches the composer’s folk‑inflected bite, while his Beethoven offers a luminous, singing line that balances classical poise with Romantic warmth. The Berg concerto, meanwhile, is delivered with a poised introspection that lets its lyrical melancholy breathe without sentimentality. The Berlin Philharmonic under Zimmermann’s leadership provides a cushion of warm, precise orchestral playing that never overshadows the solo line, yet supplies dynamic urgency where needed. The recording captures each concerto in a clear, resonant acoustic, preserving the brilliance of Zimmermann’s silvery tone and the subtle gradations of his bowing. Listeners will appreciate the way he layers each work’s distinct character without forcing a unifying interpretive scheme, allowing each composition its own voice while maintaining a cohesive programme. This disc stands out for its technical perfection, expressive depth, and the obvious simpatico between soloist and orchestra.

Editor's Choice
Anton Bruckner

Symphony No. 4, 'Romantic'

Vienna Philharmonic Orchestra / Christian Thielemann · Sony Classical
Christian Thielemann’s account of Bruckner’s Fourth Symphony, ‘Romantic’, with the Vienna Philharmonic is a magisterial reading that draws on four decades of deep familiarity with the score. From the famous opening horn call to the bustling finale, Thielemann shapes each phrase with unhurried confidence, letting the music’s monumental architecture unfold naturally while preserving its folk‑like innocence. The Vienna strings produce a velvety, warm tone that underpins the brass’ monumental surges, and the woodwinds add delicate colorations that keep the texture lively. The recording, in a generously resonant hall, captures the orchestra’s wide dynamic range, from whispered pianissimos to thunderous climaxes, without ever becoming harsh. Thielemann’s tempo choices—broad but never stagnant—grant the work a sense of timeless grandeur, making this version feel both historically informed and freshly revelatory. For anyone seeking a Bruckner Fourth that combines scholarly depth and visceral power, this disc is indispensable.

Dvořák, Kurtág

String Quintet No. 3, 'American' & String Quartets

Parker Quartet with Kim Kashkashian · ECM New Series
The Parker Quartet, joined by violist Kim Kashkashian, deliver a striking account of Dvořák’s Third String Quintet, ‘American’, paired with Kurtág’s String Quartets. In the Dvořák, the ensemble infuses the bright, dancing themes with an infectious joy while allowing the lyrical passages to breathe with heartfelt warmth; the interplay between the two violins and the viola creates a luminous texture that highlights the work’s folk influences. The Kurtág pieces, by contrast, are rendered with an intimate, exploratory sensibility, each fragment feeling like a whispered confession or a sudden outburst, and the players navigate the microtonal inflections with precision. The recording’s close‑miking balances the voices, preserving the subtle dynamic nuances that make these works feel both raw and refined. This disc stands out for its tonal clarity, emotional directness, and the obvious rapport among the musicians, offering a compelling journey from exuberance to introspection.

Editor's Choice
Arvo Pärt

Passio

Sols; Helsinki Chamber Choir / Nils Schweckendiek · BIS
Nils Schweckendiek leads the Helsinki Chamber Choir in Arvo Pärt’s Passio, delivering a deeply moving performance that balances meditative spirituality with human anguish. The choir’s diction is crystal clear, each word of the liturgical text emerging with luminous presence, while the ensemble’s pure, unified tone creates an ethereal backdrop for the soloists. The solo roles are sung with warm, focused voices that convey the narrative’s drama without exaggerated ornamentation. Schweckendiek’s pacing is unhurried yet taut, allowing the silences to speak as powerfully as the notes, and the recording captures the church acoustic in a way that gives the music an almost visual dimension. The result is a reading that feels both timeless and immediate, inviting listeners to contemplate the suffering and redemption at the heart of Pärt’s score. For those seeking a Pärt Passio that is spiritually profound yet emotionally accessible, this recording is essential.

JS Bach

Six Brandenburg Concertos, BWV1046-1051

Isabelle Faust vn Antoine Tamestit va with Akademie für Alte Musik Berlin · Harmonia Mundi · HMM90 2686/7
This second recording of the Brandenburg Concertos by the Akademie für Alte Musik Berlin celebrates their 30th anniversary with distinguished guest soloists Isabelle Faust and Antoine Tamestit joining their ranks. Rather than a star vehicle, the performances radiate communal enjoyment—Faust excels in Nos 3 and 4 while Tamestit brings warmth and ebullience to Nos 3 and 6. The recording showcases how period instrument playing standards have advanced since their 1997 version, particularly evident in Concerto No. 2's subtler shaping and natural balance. The two string concertos emerge as the most naturally exciting: the violas in No. 6 enable Tamestit and Sabine Fehlandt's warmly expressive duetting, while No. 3 flies swiftly with deft articulation that lets every counterpoint line shine. The bracing bigness of No. 5 brings rare vibrancy, and Huntgeburth's flute sounds gorgeous throughout.

Beethoven

Violin Sonatas – No. 8, Op. 30 No. 3; No. 9 'Kreutzer', Op. 47; No. 10, Op. 96

Frank Peter Zimmermann (violin), Martin Helmchen (piano) · BIS · BIS2537
Frank Peter Zimmermann and Martin Helmchen deliver a revelatory Beethoven cycle that captures both the explosive virtuosity and intimate poetry of these three violin sonatas. Helmchen's Chris Maene Straight Strung Concert Grand provides remarkable transparency, allowing both instruments to breathe even amid the Kreutzer Sonata's most turbulent passagework. Zimmermann's taut, lean lower-register tone conveys an 'electrical charge' and latent rhythmic energy, while the duo demonstrate exquisite delicacy—listen to how they lift and revolve the theme in Op. 96's opening measures, where the sound 'glints and almost dissolves.' Their Op. 30 No. 3 is 'skittish and gleeful,' and the late Op. 96 reading proves 'thoughtful, often imposing.' This cycle balances intellectual rigor with spontaneity, making every hearing feel like a fresh discovery. A triumphant conclusion to a unified and deeply satisfying traversal of Beethoven's violin literature.

JS Bach

Six English Suites, BWV806-811

Sophie Yates hpd · Chandos · CHSA0826
Sophie Yates's recording of Bach's Six English Suites stands out for her masterful synthesis of scholarly knowledge and artistic spontaneity. Performing on a replica of a 1624 Ruckers harpsichord made by Andrew Garlick, she brings fresh vitality to these Baroque masterpieces. Yates treats each suite as a self-contained universe while maintaining the cycle's cosmic coherence. Her preludes display remarkable variety—from the exploratory arpeggiation reminiscent of French clavecinistes to the orchestral richness of an Italian concerto à la Vivaldi, from the robust virtuosity of a keyboard concerto to the serious declamatory rhetoric of an organ fantasy. The dance movements reveal infinite touch and release, with interpretations that are stylistically assured yet always musically vital. What distinguishes Yates is her ability to let the composer's voice speak directly, uncluttered by modern excess. Her ornamentation grows naturally from the music's phrases, and her registrations highlight the instrument's colors without exaggeration. Patrick Rucker confidently places her among the distinguished company of Leonhardt, Egarr, and Rousset, affirming this two-disc set as a proud achievement in the English Suites discography.

Ina Boyle

Selected Songs (c.1908-1960)

Paula Murrihy mez, Robin Tritschler ten, Ben McAteer bar, Iain Burnside pf · Delphian · DCD34264
This generous 77-minute anthology presents roughly half of Irish composer Ina Boyle's complete song output, spanning six decades. The performances are exemplary throughout—Paula Murrihy's enchanting delivery of the Irish-language 'Sleep Song' and Robin Tritschler's exquisitely deft setting of Yeats's 'The Stolen Child' stand out. The programme showcases Boyle's distinctive voice: folk-inflected settings of texts from Yeats, Whitman, de la Mare, and Herbert, marked by what fellow composer Elizabeth Maconchy praised as her 'scrupulous fidelity to the mood and meaning underlying the words.' The 1913 Whitman setting 'The Last Invocation' and the pair of de la Mare songs possess a 'very real sense of wonder and flexibility.' Vivid recording with complete texts included. A most rewarding voyage of discovery that finally gives Boyle's underrecognized song legacy its due.

Josquin des Prez

Chansons (various)

Thélème / Jean-Christophe Groffe · Aparté · AP259
This magnificent anthology is the ideal Christmas gift for any Josquin enthusiast, combining first-rate performances with numerous unavailable elsewhere. Highlights include the first recording of 'Plus nulz regretz' in its correct form—a glorious, joyous celebration of the 1508 Peace of Calais—and the reconstructed bassus voice in 'Tant vous aimme.' The programme also features rarely recorded gems like 'Guillaume se va chauffer' and the motet 'Ecce tu pulchra es.' Thélème performs with four adult male voices and occasional lute, expanding to seven singers for the lament 'Nymphes des bois' to glorious effect. The ensemble adds modern touches—ondes martenot on 'La Bernardina,' Fender Rhodes on 'Mille regretz'—but wisely reserves these for the most familiar pieces. Reviewer David Fallows himself contributed the bassus reconstruction, lending scholarly authority to this essential collection that 'you cannot otherwise hear.'

Saint-Saëns

La princesse jaune

Leo Hussain (conductor)
Gramophone’s November 2021 review praises Leo Hussain’s conducting of Saint‑Saëns’s opéra comique La princesse jaune for the dramatic flair and meticulous attention he brings to the score. Hussain teases out the subtleties of the orchestration, highlighting the witty woodwind interjections, the delicate string textures and the bright brass fanfares that underpin the short, tuneful numbers. The result is a reading that feels both fresh and historically informed, giving the work’s light‑hearted plot ample room to breathe while never letting the comedy become saccharine. The singers, though not named in the excerpt, are said to deliver their roles with clarity and charm, and the ensemble playing throughout is crisp and engaging. The review notes that the recording makes a compelling case for this lesser‑known Saint‑Saëns opera, showing that it deserves a place alongside the composer’s more popular stage works. All in all, a vibrant, well‑shaped performance that rewards repeated listening.

Icons Willem Mengelberg

This Gramophone magazine feature (November 2021) commemorates the 150th anniversary of Dutch conductor Willem Mengelberg's birth. Author Rob Cowan celebrates Mengelberg as an 'icon of icons,' describing him as physically diminutive but magnetically hypnotic on the rostrum—a 'Napoleon of the orchestra' whose extensive recorded legacy remains inspirational. The article examines Mengelberg's remarkable interpretations, including Mahler's poignant 1926 Adagietto, a dramatic 1939 Palm Sunday St Matthew Passion described as 'full-blown opera,' and Tchaikovsky's symphonies (noting the evolving tempo across three Pathétique recordings from 1937-1944). Mengelberg led the Concertgebouw Orchestra from 1895-1945 and shared podium duties with Toscanini at the New York Philharmonic from 1926. The feature acknowledges controversy surrounding his conduct during the Nazi occupation, for which he was banned from conducting in Holland until 1951. Cowan highlights that Mengelberg's virtually complete recorded legacy is available online at willemmengelberg.nl, concluding with the recommendation of a 1928 recording as an essential example of Mengelberg's artistry.

The Musician And The Score

In a November 2021 Gramophone feature, violinist Jack Liebeck discusses his lifelong fascination with Eugène Ysaÿe’s six solo‑violin sonatas, focusing on the Second Sonata in A minor (subtitled ‘Obsession’). Liebeck explains that, unlike many violinists, he never enjoyed playing solo Bach, finding Ysaÿe’s writing more natural for the instrument. He describes the first movement’s quoted Bach fragment and the abrupt brutalement marking as a tongue‑in‑cheek dig at Jacques Thibaud, then explores how the Dies irae plainchant creeps through the work, giving it a gothic, monumental quality. For the muted ‘Malinconia’ Liebeck seeks a subtle, almost non‑violin colour, likening the required sound to a distant breeze or murmuring graveyard. The pizzicato sarabande of the third movement is treated as a humorous “Dance of the Shadows”, while the finale ‘Les furies’ unleashes jagged double‑stops and whirling string‑crossings that Liebeck compares to a raw, unashamed electric‑guitar solo—‘the violin equivalent of Jimi Hendrix’. Throughout the interview he stresses the importance of personal interpretation over listening to other performers, insisting that Ysaÿe’s music is pure drama, fireworks, mystery and magic. The article concludes by directing readers to the Gramophone review of Liebeck’s own Ysaÿe disc (page 62), the recording under discussion.
